\section{Model}

Let $S_t$ be the price and its evolution follows 

\begin{equation}
  dS_t = \kappa(\gamma-\log(S_t))S_t dt + \sigma S_t dZ_t.
\end{equation}

Take $X_t = \log(S_t)$, by Ito's formula,

\begin{equation}
  dX_t = \kappa(\alpha - X_t) dt + \sigma dZ_t,
\end{equation}

where $\alpha = \gamma - \frac{\sigma^2}{2\kappa}$.\\ 

Let $Q_t \in [Q_{\min},Q_{\max}]$ denote the total amount of commodity in the storage facility at time $t$ where $Q_{\min},Q_{\max}$ are the minimum and maximum capacity. Here for simplicity, take them as $0$ and $100$ separately. \\

The decisions that a facility manager can make is when and how much shall be injected and withdraw from the facility. Denote cumulative amounts of commodity purchased and sold up to time $t$ as $L_t$ and $U_t$, respectively. Here and after, buy and inject have the same meaning, so do sell and withdraw. Then 

\begin{equation}
  dQ_t = dL_t - dU_t.
\end{equation}

An admissible control policy is a pair $(L_t,U_t)$ such that $Q_t \in [Q_{\min},Q_{\max}]$ for all $t$. $\mathcal{U}$ is used to denote all admissible policies.\\

Both injection and withdraw generate costs. Let $\lambda(q)$ and $\mu(q)$ be the instantaneous costs of injection and withdrawal when the storage has $q$ unit already and $\mu(q)$ of withdrawing. Therefore, the costs of injection and withdrawal at time $t$ should be $\int_{L_{t-}}^{L_t} \lambda(q) dq$ and $\int_{U_{t-}}^{U_t} \mu(q) dq$ respectfully. We assume that $\lambda(q)$ and $\mu(q)$ are continuous and bounded. It is often easier to inject(withdraw) when empty(full) than full(empty). Therefore we assume that $\lambda(q)$ is increasing with respect to $q$ while $\mu(q)$ is decreasing.\\

The objective is to maximize discounted infinite-horizon discounted cash flows. Taking a discount factor $\beta \in (0,1)$, 

\begin{equation}\label{V(x,q)}
  V(x,q) = \max_{(L,U) \in \mathcal{U}} \mathbb{E}_{x,q} \left(\int_{0}^{\infty} e^{-\beta t}(e^{X_t} - \mu(Q^1_t))dU_t - \int_{0}^{\infty}e^{-\beta t}(e^{X_t} + \lambda(Q^2_t))dL_t\right)
\end{equation}

where $X_0 =x$ and $Q_0 = q$. $Q^1_t$ and $Q^2_t$ are introduced as following to make the objective simpler,
\begin{equation}
  Q^1_t = \mu^{-1}\left(\frac{1}{U_t - U_{t-}}\int_{U_{t-}}^{U_t} \mu(q) dq\right)
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
  Q^2_t = \lambda^{-1}\left(\frac{1}{L_t - L_{t-}}\int_{L_{t-}}^{L_t} \lambda(q) dq\right).
\end{equation}


 Since $V(x,q)$ is the maximum value that a facility manager can obtain from a storage facility, this is the value of the facility when the current spot log price is $x$ and the amount is $q$.

\section{No Transaction Costs}
No transaction cost means that $\lambda$ (buying cost) $\equiv 0$ and $\mu$ (selling cost) $\equiv 0$. Since the profit (cost) of selling (buying) one unit at the same price level is the same, the optimal strategy should be bang-bang. Whenever the drift of $X_t$ exceeds interest rate, we should buy. Vice versa. Let this critical point be $X^*$, then

\begin{equation*}
  \kappa (\alpha - X^*) = r \Rightarrow X^* = \alpha - \frac{r}{\kappa}
\end{equation*}

The optimal strategy is as following

\begin{itemize}
  \item If $X_t > X^*$, sell to empty.
  \item If $X_t \leq X^*$, buy to full.
\end{itemize}

\section{Constant Transaction Costs}
Let the constant transaction cost be $\lambda$ (buying cost) and $\mu$ (selling cost). Since the profit (cost) of selling (buying) one unit at the same price level is the same, the optimal strategy should be bang-bang. Introduce the following two functions of log price $x$,

\begin{equation*}
\begin{split}
  V(x) &= \sup_{\tau \in \mathcal{T}} \mathbb{E}_x\{e^{-r\tau}(e^{X_\tau} - \mu)\}\\
  J(x) &= \sup_{\nu \in \mathcal{T}} \mathbb{E}_x \{e^{-r\nu}(V(x^\nu) - e^{X_\nu} - \lambda) \}.
\end{split}
\end{equation*}

$V(x)$ is the maximum value that can be gained from 1 unit commodity when log price is $x$. $J(x)$ is the value of an empty storage when log price is $x$. Tim Leung et al.(2014) use concavity to prove that the optimal policy for $V(x)$ is an upper threshold policy. This means that if log price exceeds the threshold sell. $d^*$ is denoted as the threshold. Using similar idea, they also prove the optimal policy for $J(x)$ is an upper-lower threshold policy. This means that one will buy if and only if log price is inside two thresholds. $a^*$ and $b^*$ are denoted as the lower and upper threshold. Based on the existence and conditions of $a^*$ and $b^*$, there are two cases.


\begin{enumerate}
    \item $J(x) = 0$. In this case, it is not optimal enter the market at all.
    \item $-\infty < a^* < b^* < d^*< \infty$. This means that commodity is bought when log price is within $[a^*,b^*]$ and will be sold when log price achieves $d^*$.
\end{enumerate}




\section{Non Constant Transaction Costs}
\subsection{The HJB equation}
The stochastic control problem with non constant transaction costs can be transformed into a Hamilton Jacobi Bellman (HJB) equation via dynamic programming principle and Ito's formula. The HJB equation characterize the value function $V(x,q)$ (\ref{V(x,q)}).\\

We will use $V_{x}$ and $V_{q}$ separately to denote the partial differential of $V$ with respect to $x$ and $q$. $V_{xx}$ represents $\frac{\partial^2 V}{(\partial x)^2}$. The value function $V(x,q)$ is expected to satisfy the HJB equation

\begin{equation}\label{HJB}
  \max \left[ \mathcal{L} V(x,q),-V_q(x,q) + e^x - \mu(q),V_q(x,q) - e^x - \lambda(q)\right] = 0.
\end{equation}
where 
\begin{equation}\label{Loper}
  \mathcal{L} V(x,q) = \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2V_{xx}(x,q) + k(\alpha-x)V_{x}(x,q) - \beta V(x,q).
\end{equation}

Because $\mathcal{L}V(x,q)$, $-V_q(x,q) + e^x - \mu(q)$ and $V_q(x,q) - e^x - \lambda(q)$ are the profits at $(x,q)$ generated by utilizing holding, selling and buying policy respectively,  they are called holding profit, selling profit and buying profit. The region that holding profit is the highest is called the optimal holding region and denoted as $H^*$. Similarly, optimal selling (buying) region is defined and denoted as $S^*$ ($B^*$). \\

\subsection{Algorithm}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Find a large enough $M_0$ such that $S^* \supset S^0$ where the selling region $S^0 =  \{(x,q)|x\geq M_0,~Q_{\min} <q \leq Q_{\max}\}$. Set holding region $H^0 = (S^0)^c$ and buying region $B^0 = \emptyset$.   
  \item  Keep doing the following steps until either convergence or $B^n \neq \emptyset$. $n$ starts with 0. 
  \begin{enumerate}
  \item Solve the fixed boundary problem with $(H^n,S^n,B^n)$ and the solution is denoted as $V^{n}(x,q)$.
  \item Take positive maximal buying profits points as $B^{n+1}$. That is to say, $B^{n+1} = \{(x,q)|x = x_M^{n+1}(q)\}$ where
\begin{equation*}
  x_M^{n+1}(q) = \left\{
  \begin{array}{lc}
  \arg \max_{(x,q)} BP(q)& \max_{(x,q)}BP(q)>0\\
  \emptyset & \mbox{otherwise,}
  \end{array}\right.
\end{equation*}
and $BP(q) =  V^{n}_q(x,q) - e^x - \lambda(q)$.
  \item If $B^{n+1} = \emptyset$, move down the selling boundary $\partial S^n$ to the maximal selling profitable points. Mathematically speaking $S^{n+1} = \{(x,q)|x\geq x^{n+1}(q)\}$ where 
  \begin{equation*}
  x^{n+1}(q) = \arg\max_{x}\left(-V^{n}_q(x,q) + e^x - \mu(q)\right). 
  \end{equation*}

  
  \item $H^{n+1} = (S^{n+1} \cup B^{n+1})^c$.
  \item $n = n + 1$.
  \end{enumerate}
\item If previous loop ends when $n = N$ because of $B^{N} \neq \emptyset$, keep doing the following  until convergence. $n$ starts as $N$.
  \begin{enumerate}
  \item Solve the fixed boundary problem with $(H^n,S^n,B^n)$ and the solution is denoted as $V^{n}(x,q)$.
  \item If $n$ is odd, $B^{n+1} = B^{n}$. Move down the selling boundary $\partial S^n$ to the maximal selling profitable points to have $S^{n+1}$ as previously.
  \item If $n$ is even, $S^{n+1} = S^{n}$. Move up and down buying boundary $\partial B^{n+1}$ to the maximal buying profitable points. Mathematically speaking, $B^{n+1} = \{(x,q)|x_l^{n+1}(q)\leq x\leq x_u^{n+1}(q)\}$ where 
  \begin{equation*}
  \begin{split}
  x_l^{n+1}(q) &= \arg\max_{x<x_l^{n}(q)}\left(V^n_q(x,q) - e^x - \lambda(q)\right)\\
  x_u^{n+1}(q) &= \arg\max_{x>x_u^{n}(q)}\left(V^n_q(x,q) - e^x - \lambda(q)\right)\\
  x_l^{N}(q) &= x_u^{N}(q) = x_M^{N}(q)
  \end{split}
  \end{equation*}
  \item $H^{n+1} = (S^{n+1} \cup B^{n+1})^c$.
  \item $n = n + 1$. 
   \end{enumerate}
\end{enumerate}

Notice that each time only one of selling and buying boundaries moves and both selling region and buying region are increasing. These are critical in the proof of the monotonicity $V^{n}$.\\

In order to show this algorithm works, 3 things need to be proved.
\begin{enumerate}
  \item The existence of $M_0$.
  \item $V^{n}$ is monotone increasing.
  \item The boundaries can be kept moving.
\end{enumerate}


\subsection{The existence of $M_0$}

\begin{Lemma}\label{LemmaExiM_0}%existence of M_0
$\forall \q>0,~\exists M_0(\q)~ s.t.~\{(x,q)| x \geq M_0(\q), q = \q\} \subset S^*$
\end{Lemma}

\begin{proof}
First, in order to make money, for any buying price, there must be a higher selling price. Mathematically speaking, $\forall (x_1,q) \in B^*~ \exists x_2 > x_1 ~s.t.~ (x_2,q) \in S^*$.\\

If the lemma doesn't hold, combining the observation above, $\exists M_0(\q),~\q > Q_{\min}$ such that $\{(x,\q)|x>M_0(\q)\} \subset H^*$. Without losing any generality, let $M_0(\q)>\alpha$. In other words, $\forall x>M_0(\q)$ 

\begin{equation*}
\begin{split}
V_q(x,\q) & \leq e^{x} + \lambda(\q)\\
\LL V(x,\q) &=  0\\
V_q(x,\q) &\geq e^{x} - \mu(\q).\\
\end{split}
\end{equation*}
Take derivative with respect to $q$ to both sides of second equality,
\begin{equation*}
\begin{split}
V_q(x,\q) & \leq e^{x} + \lambda(\q)\\
\LL V_q(x,\q) &=  0\\
V_q(x,\q) &\geq e^{x} - \mu(\q).\\
\end{split}
\end{equation*}
Define
\begin{equation}\label{h}
h(x) = V_q(x,\q) - (e^x -\mu(\q)).
\end{equation}
Therefore, $\forall x>M_0(\q)$
\begin{equation}\label{hin}
0\leq h(x) \leq \lambda(\q) + \mu(\q).
\end{equation}
Substitute (\ref{h}) into $\LL V_q(x,\q) = 0$, 
\begin{equation*}
\frac{1}{2}\sigma^2h''(x) + k(\alpha-x)h'(x) - \beta h(x) = - \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 e^x + k (x- \alpha)e^x + \beta(e^x - \mu(\q)).
\end{equation*}
Divide both sides by $e^x$,

\begin{equation}\label{hPDE}
  \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2e^{-x}h''(x) + ke^{-x}(\alpha-x)h'(x) - \beta e^{-x}h(x) = - \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2+ k (x- \alpha) + \beta(1 - e^{-x}\mu(\q)).
\end{equation}

Because $\mu(\q)$ is bounded, right hand side of (\ref{hPDE}) goes to $+\infty$ when $x \rightarrow +\infty$. Furthermore, by (\ref{hin}), $h(x)$ is bounded. Combine these two, when $x \rightarrow +\infty$, either 

\begin{enumerate}
  \item $e^{-x}h''(x)\rightarrow +\infty$.\\
  or 
  \item $e^{-x}(\alpha-x)h'(x) \rightarrow +\infty$
\end{enumerate}


Let $t_2 > t_1 > \alpha$ and without losing any generality both $e^{-x}h''(x)$ and $e^{-x}(\alpha-x)h'(x)$ are positive on $[t_1,t_2]$. \\

In first case, by Fubini's theorem,

\begin{equation}\label{Fubini}
\begin{split}
h(t_2) - h(t_1) &= \int_{t_1}^{t_2}h'(t)dt = \int_{t_1}^{t_2}(\int_{t_1}^{t}h''(x)dx+h'(t_1))dt\\
&=  \int_{t_1}^{t_2}\int_{t_1}^{t}h''(x)dxdt +(t_2-t_1)h'(t_1)\\
&= \int_{t_1}^{t_2}\int_{x}^{t_2}h''(x)dtdx +(t_2-t_1)h'(t_1)\\
&= \int_{t_1}^{t_2}(t_2-x)h''(x)dx +(t_2-t_1)h'(t_1)\\
&\geq e^{t_1} \int_{t_1}^{t_2}(t_2-x)e^{-x}h''(x)dx + (t_2-t_1)h'(t_1) \\
& = e^{t_1} \int_{t_1}^{t_2}\left((t_2-x)e^{-x}h''(x) + e^{-t_1}h'(t_1) \right)dx.
\end{split}
\end{equation}

With $t_1$ fixed, when $t_2 \rightarrow +\infty$, the right hand side approaches positive infinity. This is a contradiction with $(\ref{hin})$.\\

In the second case,
\begin{equation}
\begin{split}
h(t_2) - h(t_1) &= \int_{t_1}^{t_2} h'(t)dt \\
&= \int_{t_1}^{t_2} \frac{e^t}{a-t} \frac{(a-t)h'(t)}{e^t}dt\\
&\leq \frac{e^{t_1}}{a-t_1} \int_{t_1}^{t_2}\frac{(a-t)h'(t)}{e^t}dt.
\end{split}
\end{equation}

With $t_1$ fixed, when $t_2 \rightarrow +\infty$, the right hand side approaches negative infinity. This is also a contradiction with $(\ref{hin})$.\\
\end{proof}

We have proved that for each $\q$, there exists a suitable $M_0(\q)$. Because discretization is used when solving the HJB equation, only finite number of $M_0(\q)$ matter. The maximal of those is $M_0$.\\

If the condition below holds, a proposed $M_0$ is large enough. Otherwise, it is not.

\begin{equation*}
  \left(-V_q(x,q) + (e^x - \mu(q))\right)_x|_{x = M_0} < 0 ~\forall q \in (Q_{\min},Q_{\max}]
\end{equation*}
 
This criterion guarantees $M_0$ can be found in practice.

\subsection{$V^{n}$ is monotone increasing}
Two lemmas are needed.

\begin{Lemma}\label{LemmaMaxPr} %maximum principle
If $\LL f = 0$, $f$ achieves the positive maximal and negative minimum at the boundary.
\end{Lemma}

\begin{proof}
Assume that there exists $x_M$ which is the positive maximal interior point. Then $f(x_M) > 0$, $f'(x_M) = 0$ and $f''(x_M) \leq 0$. On the other hand, $\LL f(x_M) = 0$. By the definition of $\LL$,

\begin{equation}
\begin{split}
  &\frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 f''(x_M) + \kappa(\alpha - x_M) f'(x_M) - \beta f(x_M) = 0 \\
  \Rightarrow & 0<\beta f(x_M) = \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 f''(x_M) + \kappa(\alpha - x_M) f' \leq 0
\end{split}
\end{equation}

Contradiction! Negative minimum case can be proved using similar method.
\end{proof}

\begin{Lemma}\label{LemmaMutEx}%mutually exclusive optimal buying and selling policies
Denote $I_{\x} = \{(x,q)|x=\x, q \in [Q_{\min},Q_{\max}]\}$. $\nexists \x$ such that both $ I_{\x} \cap S^*\neq \emptyset$ and $I_{\x} \cap B^* \neq \emptyset$ hold simultaneously.
\end{Lemma}

\begin{proof}
If not, there must exist $(\x,q_1)$ on then buying boundary and $(\x,q_2)$ on the selling boundary. 

Define
\begin{equation}\label{DFf}
f(x) = V_q(x,q_1) - (e^{x} + \lambda(q_1)).
\end{equation}

By definition, $f(\x) = 0$. If $V_q$ is continuous across boundary, $f'(\x) = 0$. By HJB equation, $f(x)$ achieves its maximum at point $\x$. Because $f(x)$ may not be second order continuous, we need to consider two cases.

\begin{enumerate}
  \item Holding region is above $(\x,q_1)$.
  \item Holding region is below $(\x,q_1)$.
\end{enumerate}

From maximal property and $f'(\x) = 0$, we have $f''(\x+) \leq 0,~f''(\x-) = 0$ for the first and $f''(\x+)= 0,~f''(\x-) \leq 0$ for the second. In the first case,

\begin{equation*}
\LL f(\x+) = \frac{1}{2} \sigma^2 f''(\x+) + k(\alpha- \x)f'(\x) -\beta f(\x) = \frac{1}{2} \sigma^2 f''(\x+) \leq 0.
\end{equation*}

On the other hand, 

\begin{equation}\label{eq3}
\begin{split}
\LL f(\x+) &= \LL V_q(\x+,q_1) - \LL(e^x + \lambda(q_1))|_{x=\x} \\
&= \left(\LL V(\x+,q_1)\right)_q- \LL(e^x + \lambda(q_1))|_{x=\x}\\
&= - \LL(e^x + \lambda(q_1))|_{x=\x}.\\
\end{split}
\end{equation}

Therefore,

\begin{equation}\label{geqbuy}
\frac{1}{2} \sigma^2 e^{\x} + k(\alpha -\x) e^{\x} - \beta (e^{\x} +\lambda(q_1))) \geq 0.
\end{equation}

In the second case, the same equality can be achieved using similar arguments above.\\

Define
\begin{equation*}
g(x) = - V_q(x,q_2) + (e^{x} - \mu(q_2)).
\end{equation*}

Replicate the same analysis for $f$ to have

\begin{equation}\label{leqsell}
\frac{1}{2} \sigma^2 e^{\x} + k(\alpha -\x) e^{\x} - \beta (e^{\x} -\mu(q_2))) \leq 0.
\end{equation}

Because $\lambda$ and $\mu$ are positive, (\ref{geqbuy}) and (\ref{leqsell}) can't hold simultaneously which is a contradiction. 

\end{proof}

\begin{Theorem}\label{incrs}
$V^{n+1} \geq V^{n}$.
\end{Theorem}

\begin{proof}

By the definitions of $(V^n,H^n,S^n,B^n)$ and the way boundary moves, we have
  
\begin{equation}\label{Vn}
\begin{array}{ll}
\LL V^{n}(x,q) = 0& (x,q) \in H^{n}\\
-V^{n}_q(x,q) + e^x - \mu(q) = 0 &(x,q) \in S^{n}\\
-V^{n}_q(x,q) + e^x - \mu(q) > 0 &(x,q) \in S^{n+1}/S^{n}\\
V^{n}_q(x,q) - e^x - \lambda(q) = 0 & (x,q) \in B^{n}\\
V^{n}_q(x,q) - e^x - \lambda(q) > 0 & (x,q) \in B^{n+1}/B^{n}.\\
\end{array}
\end{equation}

Here we also use the monotonicity of $H^n,~S^n$ and $B^n$, i.e. $H^{n} \supset H^{n+1},~S^{n} \subset S^{n+1}$ and $B^{n} \subset B^{n+1}$.\\
 
The definition of  $(V^{n+1},H^{n+1},S^{n+1},B^{n+1})$ gives us

\begin{equation}\label{Vn+1}
\begin{array}{ll}
\LL V^{n+1}(x,q) = 0& (x,q) \in H^{n+1}\\
-V^{n+1}_q(x,q) + e^x - \mu(q) = 0 &(x,q) \in S^{n}\\
-V^{n+1}_q(x,q) + e^x - \mu(q) = 0 &(x,q) \in S^{n+1}/S^{n}\\
V^{n+1}_q(x,q) - e^x - \lambda(q) = 0 & (x,q) \in B^{n}\\
V^{n+1}_q(x,q) - e^x - \lambda(q) = 0 & (x,q) \in B^{n+1}/B^{n}.\\
\end{array}
\end{equation}

Introduce $\Delta V^{n+1}(x,q) = V^{n+1}(x,q) - V^{n}(x,q)$. From (\ref{Vn}) and (\ref{Vn+1})  

\begin{equation}\label{dV}
\begin{split}
\LL \Delta V^{n+1}(x,q) &= 0 \quad (x,q) \in H^{n+1}\\
\Delta V_q^{n+1}(x,q) &= 0 \quad (x,q) \in S^{n}\\
\Delta V_q^{n+1}(x,q) &> 0 \quad (x,q) \in S^{n+1}/S^{n}\\
\Delta V_q^{n+1}(x,q) &= 0 \quad (x,q) \in B^{n}\\
\Delta V_q^{n+1}(x,q) &< 0 \quad (x,q) \in B^{n+1}/B^{n}.\\
\end{split}  
\end{equation}

Recall that in the algorithm, only one of selling and buying boundary moves. (\ref{dV}) can be further divided into two cases.

\begin{enumerate}
  \item Only selling boundary moves, namely 
\begin{equation}\label{dVS}
\begin{split}
\LL \Delta V^{n+1}(x,q) &= 0 \quad (x,q) \in H^{n+1}\\
\Delta V_q^{n+1}(x,q) &= 0 \quad (x,q) \in S^{n}\\
\Delta V_q^{n+1}(x,q) &> 0 \quad (x,q) \in S^{n+1}/S^{n}\\
\Delta V_q^{n+1}(x,q) &= 0 \quad (x,q) \in B^{n+1} = B^{n}\\
\end{split}  
\end{equation}

Take derivative with respect to $q$ in the first equality above to have 

\begin{equation}\label{dVqS}
\begin{split}
\LL \Delta V^{n+1}_q(x,q) &= 0 \quad (x,q) \in H^{n+1}\\
\Delta V_q^{n+1}(x,q) &= 0 \quad (x,q) \in S^{n}\\
\Delta V_q^{n+1}(x,q) &> 0 \quad (x,q) \in S^{n+1}/S^{n}\\
\Delta V_q^{n+1}(x,q) &= 0 \quad (x,q) \in B^{n+1} = B^{n}.\\
\end{split}  
\end{equation}

By Lemma \ref{LemmaMaxPr}, $\Delta V^{n+1}_q \geq 0$ holds for all points. Right now focus on the set $\{(x,Q_{\min})|x \in \mathbb{R}\}$. Only holding and buying are possible policy on this set because there is nothing to sell.
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Hold at $(x,Q_{\min})$. Directly from the first equality of (\ref{dVS}), we have
  \begin{equation*}
  \LL \Delta V^{n+1}(x,Q_{\min}) = 0
  \end{equation*}
  \item Buy at $(x,Q_{\min})$. By Lemma \ref{LemmaMutEx}, on this log price level $x$, there will only be holding and buying policy. Especially, we will hold at $(x,Q_{\max})$ because there is no place to inject. Therefore,
  
\begin{equation*}
  \LL \Delta V^{n+1}(x,Q_{\max}) = 0.
\end{equation*}
 
By (\ref{dVqS}),
\begin{equation*}
    \LL \Delta V^{n+1}_q(x,q) = 0 \quad \forall (x,q) \in H^{n+1}\cup B^{n+1}.
\end{equation*}

As mentioned above, only holding and buying are possible. Thanks to above equality, 

\begin{equation*}
  \int_{Q_{\min}}^{Q_{\max}}\LL \Delta V^{n+1}_q(x,q)dq = 0.
\end{equation*}

Thus,
  \begin{equation*}
  \begin{split}
  \LL \Delta V^{n+1}(x,Q_{\min}) &= \LL \left(\Delta V^{n+1}(x,Q_{\max}) - \int_{Q_{\min}}^{Q_{\max}}\Delta V^{n+1}_q(x,q)dq\right)\\
  &= \LL \Delta V^{n+1}(x,Q_{\max}) - \int_{Q_{\min}}^{Q_{\max}}\LL \Delta V^{n+1}_q(x,q)dq = 0.\\
  \end{split}
  \end{equation*}
  
\end{enumerate}

We have proved that 
\begin{equation*}
  \LL \Delta V^{n+1}(x,Q_{\min}) = 0 \quad \forall x \in \mathbb{R}.
\end{equation*}
  
This implies $ \Delta V^{n+1}(x,Q_{\min}) = 0$ because $0$ is the only finite function that satisfy above equation. Therefore,

\begin{equation*}
\begin{split}
  \Delta V^{n+1}(x,q) &= \Delta V^{n+1}(x,Q_{\min}) + \int_{Q_{\min}}^q \Delta V^{n+1}_q(x,q)dq \\
  & = \int_{Q_{\min}}^q \Delta V^{n+1}_q(x,q)dq \geq 0
\end{split}
\end{equation*}

The last inequality holds because of $\Delta V^{n+1}_q \geq 0$.

  \item Only buying boundary moves, namely
\begin{equation}\label{dVB}
\begin{split}
\LL \Delta V^{n+1}(x,q) &= 0 \quad (x,q) \in H^{n+1}\\
\Delta V_q^{n+1}(x,q) &= 0 \quad (x,q) \in S^{n+1} = S^{n}\\
\Delta V_q^{n+1}(x,q) &= 0 \quad (x,q) \in B^{n}\\
\Delta V_q^{n+1}(x,q) &< 0 \quad (x,q) \in B^{n+1}/B^{n}.\\
\end{split}  
\end{equation}

Using similar method to have $\Delta V^{n+1}_q \leq 0$ and $\Delta V^{n+1}(x,Q_{\max}) = 0$. Then

\begin{equation*}
\begin{split}
  \Delta V^{n+1}(x,q) &= \Delta V^{n+1}(x,Q_{\max}) - \int_{q}^{Q_{\max}} \Delta V^{n+1}_q(x,q)dq \\
  & = - \int_{Q_{\min}}^q \Delta V^{n+1}_q(x,q)dq \geq 0
\end{split}
\end{equation*}


\end{enumerate}

\subsection{The boundaries can keep moving}

From the algorithm, the boundaries can move if and only if the following Theorem holds.

\begin{Theorem}
Three inequalities below are true for all $n$ and $q \in [Q_{\min},Q_{\max}]$.
\begin{equation*}
\begin{split}
\left(-V_q^{n+1}(x,q) + e^{x}-\mu(q)\right)_x|_{x = x^{n+1}(q)} &\leq 0\\
\left(V_q^{n+1}(x,q) - e^{x}-\lambda(q)\right)_x|_{x = x_u^{n+1}(q)} & \geq 0\\
\left(V_q^{n+1}(x,q) - e^{x}-\lambda(q)\right)_x|_{x = x_l^{n+1}(q)} & \leq 0\\
\end{split}
\end{equation*}


\end{Theorem}

\begin{proof}
    Still only the statement for selling boundary is proved. 
    
    \begin{enumerate}
      \item In stage 1, the selling boundary is moved.
      \item In stage 2, the buying boundary is moved.
    \end{enumerate}
     
    In the first case, from previous proof, with $q$ fixed, $f(x) = \Delta V_q^{n+1}(x,q)$ achieves its maximum at point $x_S$ where $(x_S,q) \in S^{n+1}/S^{n}$. This is equivalent to 
    $f'(x_S) > 0.$\footnote{If $f'(x_s) = 0$, by $\LL f(x_s) = 0$, $0<\beta f = \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 f''(x)\leq 0$. Contradiction!} Thus, 
    
    \begin{equation}
    \begin{split}
      &(\Delta V_q^{n+1})_x(x_S,q) = f'(x_S) > 0\\
      \Rightarrow & (V_q^{n+1})_x(x_S,q) -(V_q^{n})_x(x_S,q) >0\\
      \Rightarrow & (V_q^{n+1})_x(x_S,q) - e^{x_S} > 0 \\
      \Rightarrow & \left(V_q^{n+1}(x,q) - (e^{x}-\mu(q)\right)_x|_{x = x_S} > 0 \\ 
      \Rightarrow & \left(-V_q^{n+1}(x,q) + (e^{x}-\mu(q)\right)_x|_{x = x_S} < 0.
    \end{split}
    \end{equation}
    
    Noticing that the only thing used here is that $x_S$ is the maximum. In the second case, it can be proved similarly that $x_S$ is also the maximum. See the figure (\ref{2ndstage}) for the idea. 
    
    \begin{figure}[hbt]\label{2ndstage}
      \includegraphics{deltaVstage2.eps}
      \caption{The equations that $\Delta V^{n+1}$ satisfies in the second stage.}
    \end{figure}
    
    
    Therefore we prove that we can continue moving the boundary.

\end{proof}
